# MBC Survey 2025 — Social Values Analysis
**Data:** 432 respondents (36 questions × 6 age groups × 2 genders)
**Scale:** 1 = Strongly Disagree · 3 = Neutral · 5 = Strongly Agree
**Groups:** 20s–70s × Male/Female

---

## 1. Landscape overview

### Most widely agreed-upon values

| Score | Statement |
|-------|-----------|
| 4.12 | We must leave a better world for the next generation |
| 4.12 | Fair treatment should be the first principle of legislation |
| 4.01 | Caring for the weak and vulnerable is important |
| 3.95 | Today's society is too sensitive about language |
| 3.93 | Punishing wrongdoers feels satisfying |

Despite visible political cleavages, Koreans across all demographics converge on legal fairness and intergenerational responsibility. They also broadly agree that public discourse has grown too sensitive — which, as discussed below, means very different things to different people.

### Least agreed-upon (near-universal rejection)

| Score | Statement |
|-------|-----------|
| 1.74 | I feel a thrill at natural disasters in other countries |
| 1.75 | I think society needs to completely collapse |
| 1.78 | I feel the urge to destroy beautiful things |
| 1.99 | I imagine most of humanity disappearing |

Scores this low are unusual in survey data. Destructive and nihilistic impulses register near-zero across almost every cell — though somewhat higher among younger males (see §3).

---

## 2. Generational divide (20s vs. 70s)

The largest gaps between cohorts cluster around gender roles and family structure.

| Gap (70s − 20s) | Direction | Statement |
|-----------------|-----------|-----------|
| **+1.33** | Older ↑ | Having children is a social obligation |
| **+1.10** | Older ↑ | Women should prioritize family and children |
| **+0.99** | Older ↑ | Children suffer when mothers work |
| **+0.56** | Older ↑ | We need a strong leader to suppress extremism |
| **+0.56** | Older ↑ | Compassion and sympathy are important virtues |
| **−0.52** | Younger ↑ | Going abroad instead of ancestral rites is acceptable |
| **−0.46** | Younger ↑ | Marriage is a personal choice |
| **−0.46** | Younger ↑ | Success sometimes requires pushing others down |

Traditional family expectations — motherhood as obligation, the assumed tension between women's career and family — separate the 20s from the 70s by more than a full scale point. Attitudes toward abstract equality or legislation barely shift across generations. The real generational fracture is about family norms, not political ideology.

Younger cohorts are more individualistic about marriage and personal success strategies, but not more permissive about destructive behavior. Older groups score higher on abstract pro-social values — compassion, equality, fairness — which points to these being life-course values rather than generational ones.

## 3. Gender divide (Male vs. Female)

### Top gender gaps across all ages

| M − F Gap | Male | Female | Statement |
|-----------|------|--------|-----------|
| **+0.53** | Higher | Lower | The gender wage gap has legitimate reasons |
| **+0.39** | Higher | Lower | Having children is a social obligation |
| **+0.19** | Higher | Lower | Success sometimes requires pushing others down |
| **+0.18** | Higher | Lower | Women should prioritize family and children |
| **+0.18** | Higher | Lower | Advocates for the vulnerable display uncomfortable moral superiority |
| **−0.23** | Lower | Higher | Marriage is a personal choice |
| **−0.11** | Lower | Higher | Strong regulations are needed to address climate change |

Men score higher on items that justify existing gender arrangements; women score higher on personal autonomy and climate action. The pattern holds across age cohorts.

## 4. Group profiles

### 20s Male — "Anti-PC, Meritocratic, Frustrated"

Top scores: today's society is too sensitive about language (**4.02**, highest of all 12 groups), punishing wrongdoers feels satisfying (3.92), fair treatment as the first principle of legislation (3.88).

Bottom scores: foreign natural disaster thrill (1.86), urge to destroy beautiful things (1.99), society needs to completely collapse (2.06).

The defining feature here is not nihilism — those scores are low — but discourse frustration. The anti-PC item at 4.02 is the highest single-group score in the survey. On the gender wage gap, 20s males score 3.31 vs. 20s females at 2.25, a gap of +1.06 and the largest within-cohort gender difference in the data. They are skeptical of redistribution (equal incomes = better world: 2.33) but not of fairness itself. The grievance is about rules not being applied consistently, not a rejection of rules.

---

### 20s Female — "Autonomy-Forward, Structurally Aware"

Top scores: marriage is a personal choice (**4.20**, highest of any group), today's society is too sensitive about language (3.93), caring for the weak and vulnerable (3.85).

Bottom scores: foreign natural disaster thrill (1.65), urge to destroy beautiful things (1.77), society needs to completely collapse (1.91), having children is a social obligation (**1.98**, near-universal rejection).

This group most strongly rejects traditional family obligations. They support anti-discrimination legislation at 3.45 (vs. 20s males at 3.08) and climate regulations at 3.76 (vs. 3.52). What is less obvious: their score on "advocates for the vulnerable display uncomfortable moral superiority" is 2.52, nearly identical to 20s males at 2.49. The anti-PC position is shared across young cohorts; the underlying politics are not.

20s Korean women are the most individualistic on personal life choices — marriage, children, career — and structurally aware of inequality. But not particularly radical overall. On many items they land close to their male peers.

---

### 30s Male — "Pragmatic Meritocrat"

Top scores: today's society is too sensitive about language (3.92), we must leave a better world for the next generation (3.92), fair treatment as the first principle of legislation (3.91).

Profile is close to 20s males but slightly more future-oriented. Skeptical of redistribution and luck-based accounts of wealth. Gender wage gap: 3.26 (vs. 30s females at 2.61). Marginally more accepting of traditional gender roles than the 20s cohort.

---

### 30s Female — "Values-Consistent, Punitive Fairness"

Top scores: marriage is a personal choice (4.16), punishing wrongdoers feels satisfying (**4.02**, highest of any group), today's society is too sensitive about language (3.97).

The highest retributive justice score in the data sits alongside strong fairness norms and the highest female support for adolescent discipline (3.93). They reject having children as a social obligation even more firmly than 20s women (2.05).

---

### 40s Male — "Balanced Traditionalist"

Top scores: fair treatment as the first principle of legislation (4.04), we must leave a better world for the next generation (3.94), caring for the weak and vulnerable (3.91).

The first male age group where caring for the vulnerable enters the top three. Anti-PC sentiment moderates (3.84) relative to 20–30s males. Traditional gender views start rising: mothers working → children suffer moves from 2.67 (20s males) to 3.12.

---

### 40s Female — "Empathy-Led, Individualist"

Top scores: marriage is a personal choice (4.11), we must leave a better world for the next generation (4.09), fair treatment as the first principle of legislation (4.05).

Still rejects having children as a social obligation (2.82). Climate regulation support at 3.86, among the higher female scores.

---

### 50s Male — "Fairness-Anchored Conservative"

Top scores: fair treatment as the first principle of legislation (**4.24**, highest on this item), we must leave a better world for the next generation (4.11), caring for the weak and vulnerable (4.03).

The highest legal fairness score in the data sits alongside the highest traditional gender role scores among active-age males: gender wage gap at 3.25, above neutral. Anti-PC sentiment moderates (3.84). The combination looks contradictory until you notice that it is internally consistent: procedural fairness values plus resistance to structural change in family roles is exactly what social conservatism looks like when it accepts equal rules but not equal arrangements.

---

### 50s Female — "Stable Pro-Social"

Top scores: we must leave a better world for the next generation (4.17), fair treatment as the first principle of legislation (4.16), caring for the weak and vulnerable (4.02), marriage is a personal choice (4.00).

Four items above 4.0 — the most uniformly pro-social profile in the data. The lowest "society should collapse" score of all 12 groups (1.58).

---

### 60s Male — "Principled Traditionalist"

Top scores: fair treatment as the first principle of legislation (**4.49**, highest overall), we must leave a better world for the next generation (4.45), caring for the weak and vulnerable (4.32), equal treatment for all (4.22).

The group with the highest abstract equality scores is also the most committed to traditional family roles: women should prioritize family (4.10), children suffer when mothers work (3.85), having children is a social obligation (3.79). Authoritarianism is also high (4.07). Within this worldview the positions are not contradictory — fairness, order, and traditional roles are mutually reinforcing, and the strong leader restores fairness rather than subverting it.

---

### 60s Female — "Emotionally Rich, Structure-Seeking"

Top scores: we must leave a better world for the next generation (4.42), fair treatment as the first principle of legislation (4.39), caring for the weak and vulnerable (4.32), strong leader needed to suppress extremism (**4.12**, highest female authoritarianism score).

The authoritarianism number reads more as anxiety about social disorder than as political preference. Marriage is still a personal choice at 3.89.

---

### 70s Male — "Legacy-Minded Meritocrat"

Top scores: we must leave a better world for the next generation (4.38), fair treatment as the first principle of legislation (4.34), income differences should reflect effort (**4.17**, the only group where meritocracy reaches the top three).

Traditional gender views persist but are weaker than 60s males.

---

### 70s Female — "Universalist Egalitarian"

Top scores: we must leave a better world for the next generation (4.41), caring for the weak and vulnerable (4.15), fair treatment as the first principle of legislation (4.11), equal treatment for all (4.09), income differences should reflect effort (4.08).

Both equality and meritocracy appear in the top five — a "fair work, fair reward" logic. The group most strongly rejects career over family (2.71) but shows somewhat loosened traditional family views relative to 70s males: having children as a social obligation scores 3.24 vs. 3.85.

## 5. Cross-cutting themes

### Theme A: The "anti-PC" consensus that isn't

All groups except 70s females (3.46) score "today's society is too sensitive about language" above 3.5. The number looks like consensus. Young males are responding to what they see as pro-feminist or minority-protective speech norms. Young females are responding to backlash against their own speech. Older groups are reacting to the pace of discursive change. The same score, different grievances.

### Theme B: Retributive justice

"Punishing wrongdoers feels satisfying" scores 3.93 overall, near-universally above neutral, alongside high scores for "fair treatment = first principle of legislation." Justice in this data is punitive and rule-based. That is a coherent moral position, not a pathology.

### Theme C: The gender wage gap — the sharpest fracture

Of 36 questions, the gender wage gap item produces the largest within-cohort gap in the 20s (+1.06) and the largest mean gender gap overall (+0.53). The disagreement is empirical: is the pay difference the result of legitimate factors, or structural discrimination? Men and women in the same cohort have fundamentally different answers.

### Theme D: Equality in principle, redistribution in practice

Groups that score 4.0+ on "equal treatment" and "fair legislation" score 2.47 on "the world would be better if everyone earned the same." Equality is procedural — equal rules — rather than distributive. Arguments framed around redistribution face a harder road than arguments framed around consistent rule application.

### Theme E: Social alienation is young and male

| Item | 20s M | 70s M | Gap |
|------|-------|-------|-----|
| Society needs to collapse | 2.06 | 1.58 | +0.48 |
| Urge to destroy beautiful things | 1.99 | 1.78 | +0.21 |
| Imagine humanity disappearing | 2.30 | 1.99 | +0.31 |

Still low in absolute terms. But consistently higher among young males, and they cluster alongside generational & class grievance and discourse norm frustration in the same cohort. Not a crisis. Worth watching.

---

## 6. Conclusion

South Korean social values in 2025 resist easy left–right mapping. What runs through the data instead is a tension between near-universal procedural commitments — fairness, legal equality, intergenerational responsibility — and deep disagreement over what those commitments imply in practice.

The generational divide is sharpest on the least overtly political terrain. Traditional family expectations around motherhood and marriage separate 20s from 70s by over a full scale point, while attitudes toward abstract equality barely differ across generations. The gender divide is sharpest where the disagreement is empirical: not whether women should be equal, but whether the wage gap reflects legitimate factors. On that question, 20s men and 20s women are more than a point apart.

Equality reads as procedural across the board — equal rules score above 4.0, equal outcomes below 2.5. Justice reads as retributive, consistently and broadly. The "too sensitive" item is a near-consensus number that conceals opposite frustrations depending on who is answering.

The one signal that reads differently is social alienation among young men. The absolute scores remain low. But generational & class grievance, discourse norm frustration, and mild nihilistic ideation cluster in the same cohort — and that combination is worth continued observation.

---

*Analysis based on weighted mean scores (1–5 scale) from MBCsurvey.csv. Gender gap = Male score − Female score. Age gap = 70s score − 20s score.*
