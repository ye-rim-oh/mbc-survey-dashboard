# MBC Survey 2025: What Is Your "Political Blood Type"?

- **Data:** MBC Political Blood Type quiz comparison data, based on 36 survey items by 6 age groups and 2 gender groups
- **Source note:** MBC describes the political blood type service as a political-orientation test designed on the basis of a social survey of 3,027 respondents, with comparison by gender and age ([MBC 2025](https://plood.poll-mbc.co.kr/result.html?data=DOG9NVRJ2sUYW2LqZDLCl&name=%EC%97%90%EB%AA%BD%EA%B5%B0&type=aBcD); [iMBC 2025](https://www.imbc.com/broad/enews/view.html?idx=461161))
- **Scale:** 1 = Strongly Disagree / 3 = Neutral / 5 = Strongly Agree
- **Groups:** 20s-70+ x Male/Female

---

## 1. Overall Description

### Most widely agreed-upon values

| Score | Statement                                                       |
| ----- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 4.12  | We must leave a better world for the next generation            |
| 4.11  | Fair treatment should be the first principle of legislation     |
| 4.01  | Caring for the weak and vulnerable is important                 |
| 3.95  | Our society these days tends to overreact even to a single word |
| 3.93  | It feels good when wrongdoers are caught and punished           |

The broadest consensus in this dataset centers on intergenerational responsibility, legal fairness, and care for the vulnerable. At first glance, that looks like reassuring civic common ground. Still, the consensus may be thinner than it seems. People can agree on the word "fairness" while disagreeing sharply about what it refers to. That is exactly what this survey suggests. "Fairness" is not missing in Korea. It is everywhere. The disagreement lies in what fairness is taken to require: equal rules, equal outcomes, equal recognition, or equal restraint.

Punitive justice belongs to the same moral field. The desire to see wrongdoers caught and punished is not confined to one ideological bloc. It appears as a mass sentiment. The survey, then, looks much closer to a politics of unfair treatment than to one of broad redistributive correction. Rule application, hypocrisy, and exclusion from opportunity seem more salient than egalitarian equalization. This interpretation is consistent with research distinguishing personal unfairness from broader social unfairness: the former can generate resentment about blocked opportunity and unequal treatment without necessarily producing support for egalitarian redistribution ([Kim and Hall 2024](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/00104140231193013)).

### Least agreed-upon values

| Score | Statement                                                                                |
| ----- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1.74  | I feel a thrill at natural disasters in other countries                                  |
| 1.76  | I think society needs to completely collapse                                             |
| 1.79  | I sometimes feel the urge to destroy beautiful things                                    |
| 2.00  | I sometimes imagine most of humanity disappearing, leaving only a few to start over      |
| 2.32  | Since we cannot fix social institutions, they must be torn down and rebuilt from scratch |

Openly destructive and nihilistic items remain low across the board. The survey does not depict a society in generalized moral breakdown. Even so, these items are not randomly distributed. They are consistently somewhat higher among younger men. So the issue is not whether alienation dominates Korean public life. It does not. The issue is whether alienation clusters with other grievances in one part of the age-gender map. It does.

### Largest generational gaps, 70+ minus 20s

| Gap, 70+ minus 20s | Direction      | Statement                                                                                       |
| ------------------ | -------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **+1.53**          | Older higher   | Having children is a social obligation                                                          |
| **+1.29**          | Older higher   | Work is fine, but women should prioritize family and children                                   |
| **+1.10**          | Older higher   | Children suffer when mothers work                                                               |
| **+0.75**          | Older higher   | Compassion and sympathy for those who suffer are the most important human virtues               |
| **+0.69**          | Older higher   | We need a strong leader to suppress the extremism that is ruining our country                   |
| **-0.86**          | Younger higher | Going abroad on holiday breaks without holding memorial rites is not something to be criticized |
| **-0.76**          | Younger higher | Marriage is not an obligation, it is a personal choice                                          |
| **-0.68**          | Younger higher | Success sometimes requires pushing others down                                                  |

The strongest age cleavage is not abstract democracy, equal treatment, or even redistribution. It is family obligation. That is the central empirical fact of this survey. Democratic language may be shared across generations without producing similar convergence on family and gender relations. That is the pattern on display here.

There is also a life-course component. Older cohorts are not simply more authoritarian. They are more duty-oriented, more compassionate, and more inclined to see order and care as mutually reinforcing. Gendered domestic expectations seem to remain sticky because family labor is judged through moral standards as much as through preference. Experimental research on housework norms shows that gendered expectations shape moral judgments, perceived social consequences, and allocations of responsibility in household contexts ([Thébaud, Kornrich, and Ruppanner 2021](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0049124119852395)). The Korean age gradient in this dataset fits that broader account.

### Largest gender gaps, Male minus Female

| M minus F Gap | Male   | Female | Statement                                                                                                                                                                         |
| ------------- | ------ | ------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **+0.66**     | Higher | Lower  | The gender wage gap has legitimate reasons                                                                                                                                        |
| **+0.44**     | Higher | Lower  | Having children is a social obligation                                                                                                                                            |
| **+0.25**     | Higher | Lower  | Success sometimes requires pushing others down                                                                                                                                    |

| **+0.20**     | Higher | Lower  | Work is fine, but women should prioritize family and children                                                                                                                     |
| **-0.30**     | Lower  | Higher | Marriage is not an obligation, it is a personal choice                                                                                                                            |
| **-0.26**     | Lower  | Higher | Going abroad on holiday breaks without holding memorial rites is not something to be criticized                                                                                   |

The sharpest male-female fracture concerns not a general principle but a concrete social diagnosis: whether the gender wage gap is legitimate. That makes the disagreement harder to resolve. The split is not only normative. It also turns on causal interpretation. Survey evidence on South Korean attitudes toward gender discrimination shows that men and women diverge over whether gender discrimination exists and over which gender is disadvantaged ([Coyle, White, and Rich 2023](https://thediplomat.com/2023/10/how-do-south-koreans-view-gender-discrimination/)).

Among younger voters, the Korean gender conflict appears closely tied to economic insecurity, housing strain, and a sense that feminist claims compete over scarce opportunity. Lee argues that anti-gender politics among young Korean men is rooted in the interaction between economic insecurity, traditional familism, and perceived disadvantage amid women's advancement in education and the labor market ([Lee 2025](https://academic.oup.com/sp/article/32/3/584/7826751)). The survey matches that picture closely. Men score higher on items that legitimate existing gender arrangements. Women score higher on autonomy and the rejection of family obligation. The divide therefore looks durable rather than episodic.

### A first reading of Korea's current value structure

Taken together, the survey depicts a society with strong commitments to procedural fairness and generational responsibility, wide support for punitive justice, and deep disagreement over family roles and gendered causality. That combination does not fit a clean left-right map. It is better described as a conflict between universal procedural language and sharply particular social interpretations.

The redistribution items make the point especially clear. Respondents who score very high on equal treatment and fair legislation still score low on "the world would be better if everyone earned the same." This dataset suggests that unfair process matters more than unequal outcomes as such. Korean respondents seem much more responsive to unfair treatment than to equalization.

This is also why the shared moral vocabulary may not reduce polarization by itself. Research on moralized values suggests that value disagreement becomes especially polarizing when the relevant value is treated as morally binding ([Jung and Clifford 2025](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/american-political-science-review/article/varieties-of-values-moral-values-are-uniquely-divisive/B4A9A0169B28268CA0BA8850F86BE737)). Fairness may therefore operate as both common language and conflict amplifier.

## 2. Age x Gender Profiles

### 20s Male

Top scores: "Our society these days tends to overreact even to a single word" (**3.97**, highest of any group on this item), "Marriage is not an obligation, it is a personal choice" (3.90), "It feels good when wrongdoers are caught and punished" (3.89).

Frustration is loudest, and most politically combustible, in this cohort. The profile is not conventionally traditional. "Marriage is not an obligation, it is a personal choice" still scores highly. Yet the group is strongly anti-paternalistic in discourse, skeptical of redistributive correction, and highly sensitive to perceived hypocrisy. On "The gender wage gap has legitimate reasons," 20s men score 3.31 against 20s women at 2.13, the largest within-cohort split in the data. Economic insecurity and blocked life chances likely make anti-feminist appeals more persuasive when they are framed as fairness claims rather than as explicit hierarchy ([Lee 2025](https://academic.oup.com/sp/article/32/3/584/7826751)).

### 20s Female

Top scores: "Marriage is not an obligation, it is a personal choice" (**4.33**, highest of any group), "Our society these days tends to overreact even to a single word" (3.93), "Going abroad on holiday breaks without holding memorial rites is not something to be criticized" (3.91).

Bottom scores: "Having children is a social obligation" (**2.07**), "I think society needs to completely collapse" (1.91), "I sometimes feel the urge to destroy beautiful things" (1.77).

This is the most autonomy-forward profile in the dataset. Family obligation is rejected more decisively here than anywhere else. Yet anti-PC sentiment is still high. That matters because the same score can encode very different grievances. For this group, resistance to speech policing looks less like a defense of old hierarchy than a defense of self-determination. Autonomy here looks more social and everyday than party-centered.

### 30s Male

Top scores: "Our society these days tends to overreact even to a single word" (3.96), "Marriage is not an obligation, it is a personal choice" (3.93), "Fair treatment should be the first principle of legislation" (3.92).

The 30s male profile looks like a moderated version of 20s men. Anti-PC sentiment remains high, but procedural fairness moves closer to the center of gravity. Traditional gender views are still present: "The gender wage gap has legitimate reasons" remains high at 3.26. Overall, the pattern is pragmatic rather than nihilistic. Grievance is still there, but it is less raw and more institutionalized.

### 30s Female

Top scores: "Marriage is not an obligation, it is a personal choice" (4.29), "It feels good when wrongdoers are caught and punished" (**4.05**, among the highest of any group on this item), "Adolescents need more discipline" (3.99).

This is one of the clearest cases where proceduralism and punitiveness coexist. The combination can look contradictory if fairness is assumed to mean leniency or social-democratic correction. Here it does not. Order, discipline, and fairness travel together. Family obligation remains low at 2.09, placing this cohort much closer to younger women than to older generations on gender roles.

### 40s Male

Top scores: "Fair treatment should be the first principle of legislation" (4.04), "It feels good when wrongdoers are caught and punished" (3.93), "Caring for the weak and vulnerable is important" (3.89).

This is the first male cohort in which care enters the top tier without displacing punitive justice. The anti-PC edge softens relative to younger men, but traditional family expectations begin to rise. "Children suffer when mothers work" moves from 2.67 in 20s men to 3.10 here. Compared with younger men, this cohort looks less aggrieved and more invested in balancing compassion with order.

### 40s Female

Top scores: "Marriage is not an obligation, it is a personal choice" (4.27), "We must leave a better world for the next generation" (4.11), "Fair treatment should be the first principle of legislation" (4.08).

This profile combines personal autonomy with civic stewardship. Traditional family obligation remains clearly below neutral at 2.85, but the tone is less oppositional than among younger women. It is a stable, institution-facing version of autonomy politics: less movement-like, more norm-setting.

### 50s Male

Top scores: "Fair treatment should be the first principle of legislation" (**4.25**, highest on this item), "We must leave a better world for the next generation" (4.12), "Caring for the weak and vulnerable is important" (4.06).

This is one of the most internally coherent conservative profiles in the survey. Procedural fairness, intergenerational responsibility, and care all score high, while traditional gender-role items also rise. The combination suggests not hypocrisy but a particular moral ordering: equal rules, strong obligations, and skepticism toward structural revision of family life.

### 50s Female

Top scores: "We must leave a better world for the next generation" (4.17), "Fair treatment should be the first principle of legislation" (4.15), "Marriage is not an obligation, it is a personal choice" (4.09).

Among active-age groups, this is the most consistently pro-social and least volatile profile. Three items sit above 4.0. Alienation items remain very low. The group looks civically stable, not especially polarized, and still more autonomy-friendly than same-age men.

### 60s Male

Top scores: "Fair treatment should be the first principle of legislation" (**4.52**, highest overall), "We must leave a better world for the next generation" (4.47), "Caring for the weak and vulnerable is important" (4.37).

This is the most normatively saturated profile in the dataset. It combines the strongest procedural and care commitments with strong traditionalism: "Work is fine, but women should prioritize family and children" (4.25), "Having children is a social obligation" (3.85), and "We need a strong leader to suppress the extremism that is ruining our country" (4.31). The pattern matters because high fairness scores do not automatically imply liberal individualism. In this cohort, fairness, duty, and hierarchy reinforce one another rather than pull apart.

### 60s Female

Top scores: "We must leave a better world for the next generation" (4.47), "Caring for the weak and vulnerable is important" (4.36), "Fair treatment should be the first principle of legislation" (4.36).

The 60s female profile shares much of the older moral vocabulary of care, order, and responsibility, but with a somewhat looser attachment to family traditionalism than 60s men. "Marriage is not an obligation, it is a personal choice" still scores 3.91. The result is not liberalism in the younger-cohort sense, but it is clearly less patriarchal than the male counterpart.

### 70+ Male

Top scores: "We must leave a better world for the next generation" (4.42), "Fair treatment should be the first principle of legislation" (4.36), "Caring for the weak and vulnerable is important" (4.30).

Traditionalism persists, though more mildly than among 60s men. The dominant tone is legacy-minded moral stewardship rather than conflict. The group still leans toward family obligation, but its strongest commitments are civic rather than combative.

### 70+ Female

Top scores: "We must leave a better world for the next generation" (4.47), "Caring for the weak and vulnerable is important" (4.18), "Equal treatment for all people is important" (4.15).

This is the most universalist profile among the oldest respondents. Even here, family obligation is not absent, but it is clearly looser than among same-age men: "Having children is a social obligation" scores 3.24 versus 3.86 for 70+ men. That gap matters because it shows that gender differentiation survives even inside the oldest generation.

## 3. Descriptive Regression Analysis

To reduce the 36 items to a smaller descriptive map, I build five simple indices: **traditional family norms**, **gender inequality skepticism**, **procedural fairness**, **authoritarian order**, and **alienation**. The gender index combines agreement that the gender wage gap has legitimate reasons with reverse-coded support for an anti-discrimination law. For each index, I fit a cell-level linear regression using the 12 age-gender cells:

`IndexScore_i = beta_0 + beta_1 AgeStep_i + beta_2 Female_i + beta_3 (AgeStep_i x Female_i) + epsilon_i`

Here, `AgeStep` is age measured in decades from the 20s group, and `Female = 1` for women and `0` for men. Because the source data are grouped means rather than individual-level responses, and because cell sample sizes and sampling weights are not available, these regressions are descriptive only. The coefficients summarize gradients across the 12 observed cells. They should not be interpreted as causal estimates or as full population-level statistical inference.

For that reason, I do not emphasize conventional significance stars. The table below reports coefficients and standard errors only as a compact way to describe the fitted cell-level pattern.

### Fitted profiles from the descriptive regressions

| Index                        | 20s M | 20s F | 70+ M | 70+ F |
| ---------------------------- | ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| Traditional family norms     | 2.62  | 2.08  | 3.75  | 3.77  |
| Gender inequality skepticism | 3.15  | 2.36  | 2.94  | 3.00  |
| Procedural fairness          | 3.60  | 3.75  | 4.33  | 4.17  |
| Authoritarian order          | 3.63  | 3.74  | 4.08  | 3.93  |
| Alienation                   | 2.15  | 1.87  | 1.70  | 1.69  |

*Entries are OLS coefficients with standard errors in parentheses. Since the model is fitted to grouped cell means rather than individual-level data, the table should be read descriptively.*

### Descriptively clear patterns

The age slope is positive for `Traditional family norms`, `Procedural fairness`, and `Authoritarian order`, while it is negative for `Alienation`. That is the most stable age pattern in the models. By contrast, the age slope for `Gender inequality skepticism` is small among men.

The 20s gender gap is also concentrated in a few domains. It is negative for `Traditional family norms`, `Gender inequality skepticism`, and `Alienation`, which means 20s women score lower than 20s men on those indices. The female gap is smaller for `Procedural fairness` and `Authoritarian order`.

The interaction term matters descriptively for three indices. Women move upward with age faster than men on `Traditional family norms` and `Gender inequality skepticism`, which is why those youth gender gaps narrow with age. On `Authoritarian order`, the female age gradient is flatter than the male one. `Procedural fairness` shows a modestly flatter female age gradient, while `Alienation` shows little gender difference in the age slope.

Traditional family norms remain the clearest age cleavage in the data. Among men, the fitted score rises by about **0.22 points per decade**, and the positive interaction term means women move upward with age even faster. What begins as a sharp youth gender divide ends in near-convergence at the top: both 70+ men and 70+ women sit near 3.75. This is still the cleanest quantitative summary of the survey. The deepest line of division is not abstract equality. It is obligation inside the family.

The gender inequality skepticism index reveals a different structure. The widest gap appears in the 20s, where men score **3.15** and women **2.36**. By the 70+ group the gap is almost gone, with men at **2.94** and women at **3.00**. In other words, the strongest gender fracture is concentrated among younger respondents, not spread evenly across the age distribution. The survey suggests that disagreement is most acute among younger men and women, then softens with age.

Procedural fairness shows the opposite pattern. It is high across the entire age-gender space and rises with age, but it is only weakly gendered. Even 20s men start at **3.60**. By the 70+ group, men reach **4.33** and women **4.17**. The survey therefore cannot be read as simple moral decay or ideological radicalization. There is a broad procedural core. Conflict begins after that shared commitment, when groups attach different meanings to wages, care work, social protection, and public recognition.

Authoritarian order is not a residual preference confined to older conservatives. It starts high even among younger respondents, with fitted scores of **3.63** for 20s men and **3.74** for 20s women. Older men still reach the highest level at **4.08**, but the broader point is that discipline, punishment, and strong leadership travel well beyond one demographic bloc. The age slope is positive for both genders, though somewhat steeper for men. In this dataset, order is a widely available moral language rather than a niche attachment.

Alienation remains the one dimension most concentrated among the young, especially young men. The fitted score falls from **2.15** among 20s men to **1.70** among 70+ men, and from **1.87** to **1.69** among women. The levels are low in absolute terms, but the pattern survives aggregation into a broader index. Alienation is therefore the only dimension in the survey that still looks distinctly cohort-specific after values are compressed into broader themes.

## 4. Discussion

### Procedural fairness without distributive egalitarianism

One of the clearest features of the survey is the coexistence of strong procedural universalism with weak enthusiasm for equal outcomes. That should not be treated as confusion. Kim and Hall draw a useful distinction here. Personal unfairness and social unfairness do not point in the same political direction. The former generates resentment about unequal treatment, hypocrisy, and blocked opportunity. The latter is more closely associated with concern about the unfairness of society's distributional structure ([Kim and Hall 2024](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/00104140231193013)).

Cusack, Iversen, and Rehm help explain why that matters. Redistribution is tied to skill structure and labor-market risk exposure, not just moral preference ([Cusack, Iversen, and Rehm 2006](https://doi.org/10.1093/oxrep/grj004)). Hajdu similarly shows that perceived unfairness of income distribution and perceived inequality are analytically distinct, and that perceived unfairness is closely related to subjective social status ([Hajdu 2025](https://academic.oup.com/ser/article-abstract/23/2/955/7754855)). Put differently, people may feel wronged by the game without wanting to rewrite the whole distribution of rewards. That is the pattern visible here: equality before rules is popular, equalization of outcomes is not.

### Family obligation remains the main age cleavage

The regression results make one point hard to miss. The strongest age divide is not about democracy, redistribution, or even punishment. It is about family obligation. Older respondents are much more willing to treat childbearing, maternal care, and gendered domestic roles as moral duties rather than lifestyle choices. Thébaud, Kornrich, and Ruppanner offer a useful frame: housework and care norms persist not only because of economic specialization but because people attach moral judgments and social consequences to gendered household behavior ([Thébaud, Kornrich, and Ruppanner 2021](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0049124119852395)). The Korean age gradient fits that account well.

This also helps explain why generational change in Korea can look uneven. Support for equal treatment rises across the age structure, but the underlying meaning of social obligation does not move in parallel. This pattern is consistent with Go's argument that differences in democratic consolidation shaped the trajectory of gender equality differently in South Korea and Taiwan ([Go 2025](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-east-asian-studies/article/political-roots-of-gender-divergence-democratic-consolidation-and-gender-equality-in-taiwan-and-south-korea/85A598FE19D3063966917C703DDBA51F)). The family index in this survey sits comfortably within that broader account.

### Gender conflict is concentrated among the young

The gender skepticism index shows that the sharpest fracture sits among younger men and women. It is built from only two directly relevant items, but both point in the same direction: whether the gender wage gap reflects legitimate differences and whether anti-discrimination legislation is warranted. The resulting split is largest in the 20s and then narrows steadily with age. This shifts the story away from a vague claim that "men and women disagree" and toward a more specific claim that younger men and younger women are living inside rival accounts of fairness.

Coyle, White, and Rich show that South Koreans disagree not only over whether gender discrimination exists but also over who is disadvantaged by it ([Coyle, White, and Rich 2023](https://thediplomat.com/2023/10/how-do-south-koreans-view-gender-discrimination/)). Lee explains why that disagreement became politically combustible. Economic insecurity, housing pressure, and delayed family formation make gender equality claims easier to cast as zero-sum competition over scarce chances ([Lee 2025](https://academic.oup.com/sp/article/32/3/584/7826751)). The survey captures that structure clearly. Younger women reject family obligation most strongly. Younger men are much more willing to treat existing gender inequality as legitimate.

### Order is broad, not marginal

The authoritarian order index adds another layer. Strong leadership, discipline, and punishment are not fringe sentiments in this dataset. They are mainstream. Older men score highest, but younger women are also relatively high because they score strongly on discipline and punitive justice. The pattern complicates any simple contrast between liberal youth and authoritarian elders. The Korean value structure is tougher-minded than that contrast suggests.

Han provides broader context here rather than a direct explanation of these mass attitudes. His analysis of 17 years of South Korean National Assembly standing-committee minutes finds evidence of elite polarization using natural language processing methods ([Han 2022](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-east-asian-studies/article/elite-polarization-in-south-korea-evidence-from-a-natural-language-processing-model/09D7430A00F4FAF085751A9594BD4DDC)). If elite polarization has become more durable and less cyclical, it may make order-oriented language more politically available across competing camps. That is not direct evidence about public opinion in this dataset, but it helps situate why demands for discipline and strong leadership can remain salient without collapsing into a single ideological bloc.

### Alienation is low, but it is not random

The alienation items remain low enough that any grand theory of social breakdown would be overstated. Still, their clustering among younger men should not be ignored. Lee links young male backlash to labor-market precarity, housing costs, and blocked mobility ([Lee 2025](https://academic.oup.com/sp/article/32/3/584/7826751)). Cremaschi, Bariletto, and De Vries show in another context that collective economic shocks become politically consequential when they interact with preexisting narratives of public-service deprivation and state neglect ([Cremaschi, Bariletto, and De Vries 2025](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/american-political-science-review/article/without-roots-the-political-consequences-of-collective-economic-shocks/1BA32999CD907689CA66A6CB06B8519B)). The Korean case is not the same, but the mechanism is suggestive. Mild nihilistic sentiment becomes more politically meaningful when it appears next to grievance about fairness, competition, and exclusion from social recognition.

The broader implication is straightforward. Korea still has a large shared procedural vocabulary. What it lacks is a shared interpretation of who is burdened, who is protected, and which inequalities count as legitimate. Alienation matters because it is one way those unresolved conflicts become emotionally charged in a specific part of the age-gender map.

## 5. Conclusion

The overall picture is not one of ideological chaos. It is a structured value landscape. Koreans in this survey broadly endorse fairness, care, intergenerational duty, and punishment for wrongdoing. Those commitments are real, but they do not settle the political conflicts that matter most. The sharpest disagreements concern family obligation, gender inequality, and the meaning of legitimate order.

The five-index regression sharpens that point. Traditional family norms show the strongest age gradient. Gender inequality skepticism shows the strongest young male-female split. Procedural fairness remains broadly shared. Authoritarian order is high across much of the age-gender map rather than isolated at one pole. Alienation stays low, but it is still concentrated among younger men. Taken together, these results do not fit a clean left-right story. Instead, they point to a common procedural vocabulary attached to rival social interpretations of what fairness demands.

If that reading is right, Korean polarization may deepen because citizens attach incompatible meanings to a shared moral language, not because that language is absent. That is a harder political problem. Appeals to fairness alone will not resolve it, because fairness is already the shared term. The conflict is over whose experience, whose burden, and whose injury counts as unfair.
